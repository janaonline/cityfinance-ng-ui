What is the Property Tax Digital Toolkit?
This toolkit is a one-stop-shop for all your needs for reforming property taxes in your State/ULB. It provides reform recommendations based on best-practices implemented nationally and internationally and provides a step-wise implementation guide that you can easily follow to take immediate action. Reform recommendations in each stage of the lifecycle of property tax have been framed as responses to issues and implementation challenges, sharply addressing WHAT NEEDS to BE DONE and HOW CAN IT BE DONE.
Valuation
Current Practices

Assessment of properties refers to the exercise of assessing the value of a particular property within the parameters defined by ULB or State for the purpose of taxation. The rules and formulae for assigning value to all properties within the city are defined by valuation. Assessment is the application of those rules and formulas to individual properties. The strength of the assessment process can be judged by the completeness and veracity of the assessment records. The process of assessment has three aspects to it –
1. Assessment methodology used for assessing property tax payable by individual properties
2. Exemptions as defined by statutory provisions
3. Dispute resolution mechanisms

Current Practices

Collection efficiency of property tax, which is an important own revenue indicator and is calculated as the ratio of tax collected to demand raised, is dependent on both completeness of billing and administrative efficiencies in the collection process. While many ULBs do not follow this, it is a good practice to ensure that all properties that have been assessed are billed, tax is collected in a timely manner, and periodic payment reminders are issued especially using electronic modes such as SMS and/or emails. The practice of maintaining manual records, which is the case for several ULBs in India, is also a key impediment to efficient billing & collection as it is vulnerable to errors. Significant staff vacancies in revenue departments also adversely impact collection efficiencies. Although some cities have done well to create online billing and collection systems through services of external vendors, they face difficulties in managing the system due to inadequate capacity building measures being undertaken for revenue officials. Online/digital payment of property tax is another good practice whose adoption has been relatively slow and varies across cities. For instance, Coimbatore has a relatively strong property tax system, but a very low rate of adoption of online payment – 14% in FY 2021-22 year.

Current Practices

Property Tax Management Information System (MIS) exists in all ULBs that have an online property register integrated with payment channels. However, the MIS systems have generally not been integrated with decision-making to improve coverage and collections. There are very few examples of States in which MIS systems are used by different levels of Municipal and State authorities for decision-making as well as monitoring and managing performance. Property tax collection and pendency (defaulters) data is also not available in public domain in majority of the ULBs.

Most ULBs also lack an ‘incentives mechanism’ which recognizes and rewards/ awards above-par revenue officials. Without systematic and systemic tracking of property tax revenue collections, and an institutional arrangement of rewarding better performing revenue officials, the revenue collections of ULBs are likely to remain sub-optimal.
